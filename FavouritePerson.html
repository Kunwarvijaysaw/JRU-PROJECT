My Favourite Person

DR. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam

Born on 15th October 1931 at Rameswaram in Tamil Nadu, Dr. Avul Pakir Jainulabdeen Abdul Kalam, specialized in Aeronautical Engineering from Madras Institute of Technology. Dr. Kalam made significant contribution as Project Director to develop India's first indigenous Satellite Launch Vehicle (SLV-III) which successfully injected the Rohini satellite in the near earth orbit in July 1980 and made India an exclusive member of Space Club. He was responsible for the evolution of ISRO's launch vehicle programme, particularly the PSLV configuration. After working for two decades in ISRO and mastering launch vehicle technologies, Dr. Kalam took up the responsibility of developing Indigenous Guided Missiles at Defence Research and Development Organisation as the Chief Executive of Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP). He was responsible for the development and operationalisation of AGNI and PRITHVI Missiles and for building indigenous capability in critical technologies through networking of multiple institutions. He was the Scientific Adviser to Defence Minister and Secretary, Department of Defence Research & Development from July 1992 to December 1999. During this period he led to the weaponisation of strategic missile systems and the Pokhran-II nuclear tests in collaboration with Department of Atomic Energy, which made India a nuclear weapon State. He also gave thrust to self-reliance in defence systems by progressing multiple development tasks and mission projects such as Light Combat Aircraft. As Chairman of Technology Information, Forecasting and Assessment Council (TIFAC) and as an eminent scientist, he led the country with the help of 500 experts to arrive at Technology Vision 2020 giving a road map for transforming India from the present developing status to a developed nation. Dr. Kalam has served as the Principal Scientific Advisor to the Government of India, in the rank of Cabinet Minister, from November 1999 to November 2001 and was responsible for evolving policies, strategies and missions for many development applications. Dr. Kalam was also the Chairman, Ex-officio, of the Scientific Advisory Committee to the Cabinet (SAC-C) and piloted India Millennium Mission 2020.

Mahatma Gandhi

Mahatma Gandhi, born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, Gujarat, was a prominent leader of India’s independence movement and is revered as the "Father of the Nation." Deeply influenced by his mother’s religious beliefs and Jain values of non-violence (Ahimsa), Gandhi carried these principles throughout his life. After studying law in London, he moved to South Africa in 1896, where he witnessed racial discrimination and began his journey of social and political activism using nonviolent resistance or *Satyagraha*. Returning to India, he led mass movements such as the Non-Cooperation Movement, Salt March, and Quit India Movement, advocating peaceful protest against British rule. Gandhi was imprisoned multiple times but remained committed to truth, simplicity, and social reform, especially in fighting untouchability and promoting rural development and khadi. He played a key role in mobilizing Indian society and uniting people across religions and castes. His refusal to support violence, even during World War II, brought both praise and criticism. Gandhi opposed the partition of India but stepped aside as younger leaders like Nehru and Patel supported it. In his final years, he worked tirelessly to stop communal violence, fasting to promote Hindu-Muslim unity. On January 30, 1948, he was assassinated in Delhi by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu extremist. Gandhi's life, rooted in spiritual strength and moral courage, inspired global leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela, and his philosophy of nonviolence remains a guiding light for peace and justice movements around the world.

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar

Dr. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar, popularly known as Babasaheb Ambedkar, was born on April 14, 1891, in Mhow (now in Madhya Pradesh) as the 14th and last child of his parents. His father, Subedar Ramji Maloji Sakpal, was in the British Army and a follower of Sant Kabir. Born into an untouchable caste, Ambedkar faced social discrimination from childhood. He received his early education in Bombay and later graduated from Elphinstone College with the support of a scholarship from the Maharaja of Baroda. Selected for higher studies, he earned M.A. and Ph.D. degrees from Columbia University, and later a D.Sc. and Bar-at-Law from London. Despite his outstanding qualifications, caste discrimination persisted even after returning to India. He took up various academic roles, practiced law, and founded institutions for the upliftment of the depressed classes. In 1924, he established the Bahishkrit Hitkarini Sabha to promote education and social reforms for Dalits. He was a powerful orator, professor, economist, and social reformer who fearlessly challenged the caste system. In 1935, at Yeola, he publicly declared he would not die a Hindu, and in 1956, he embraced Buddhism along with thousands of his followers. He served as the Labour Member in the Viceroy’s Executive Council in 1942 and was elected to the Constituent Assembly in 1946, playing a key role in drafting the Indian Constitution, earning him the title "Architect of the Indian Constitution." After independence, he became the first Law Minister of India but resigned in 1951 over differences with Nehru. He wrote extensively, including *The Annihilation of Caste*, *Who Were the Shudras?*, and *Thoughts on Linguistic States*. He was honored with the title of “Bodhisattva” by Buddhist monks in 1954. He also contributed to the foundation of the Reserve Bank of India. On October 14, 1956, he converted to Buddhism, and he passed away on December 6, 1956. His death anniversary is observed as *Mahaparinirvan Diwas*. Dr. Ambedkar dedicated his life to fighting social injustice, promoting equality, and uplifting the marginalized. His life and ideas remain relevant today as India continues to face challenges of social and economic inequality. His intellectual legacy and relentless struggle for justice make him one of the greatest figures in modern Indian history.

Bhagat Singh

Bhagat Singh, one of India’s most iconic revolutionaries, was born on September 28, 1907, in Banga village, Punjab (now in Pakistan), into a patriotic Sikh family involved in India’s freedom struggle. Influenced from a young age by his family’s nationalist ideals and the Jallianwala Bagh massacre of 1919, he grew up with deep resentment against British colonial rule. He joined the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) and believed that only a revolutionary approach could free India. Bhagat Singh strongly opposed the non-violent approach of some leaders and instead supported direct action to inspire the masses and shake the foundations of British authority. In 1928, to avenge the death of freedom fighter Lala Lajpat Rai—who had been assaulted during a peaceful protest—Bhagat Singh, along with Rajguru and Sukhdev, planned and executed the killing of British police officer John Saunders, mistakenly instead of Scott, the officer responsible. To spread awareness, he and Batukeshwar Dutt later threw non-lethal bombs in the Central Legislative Assembly on April 8, 1929, shouting slogans like “Inquilab Zindabad” and surrendering willingly. During his trial and imprisonment, he used the courtroom and his writings to spread revolutionary ideas and highlight British injustices. While in jail, he demanded equal treatment for political prisoners and undertook a historic hunger strike. Bhagat Singh was not only a fearless revolutionary but also a thinker and writer, deeply influenced by socialism, Marxism, and anti-colonial ideas. He read extensively, including works by Marx, Lenin, and other political philosophers. His essay Why I am an Atheist reveals his rational thinking and deep commitment to human values. Despite widespread support and appeals for clemency, Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev were executed on March 23, 1931, in Lahore Central Jail at just 23 years of age. Their execution sparked nationwide protests and further fueled the freedom movement. Bhagat Singh became a martyr and symbol of youthful courage, sacrifice, and revolutionary idealism. His life and death continue to inspire generations, especially youth, to fight injustice and stand for truth. Bhagat Singh remains a national hero, remembered for his fearless defiance of colonial rule and his vision of an India based on equality, freedom, and justice. His martyrdom day, March 23, is observed as Shaheed Diwas across India in his honor.

Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose

Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose was an Indian nationalist who led (Second Phase) the Indian National Army (INA) in the independence movement. He was a prominent leader of the INC. Subhas Chandra Bose served as the President of the party for two tenures and the 5th Mayor of Calcutta (now Kolkata). Netaji was the first Indian who traveled in a submarine from Kiel to Tokyo via Singapore. He was one of the greatest freedom fighters of India who escaped under the nose of the Britishers.
